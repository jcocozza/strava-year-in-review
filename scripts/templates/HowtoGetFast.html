Mileage

Mileage philosophy is pretty simple. Run as much as you can consistently maintain. As a general rule of thumb, more mileage is better– PROVIDED YOU CAN SUSTAIN IT.

Although it isn’t always good to set strict mileage goals, it’s worth noting that professional runners are running well over 90 miles per week. While I don’t recommend anyone tries to emulate the training of professional runners, this website provides training information for many well known professional distance runners. Of notable interest: Haile Gebrselassie and Eliud Kipchoge.

The Lactate Threshold Training Paradigm

The limiting performance factor for most 5k/10 runners is the anaerobic threshold. Speed matters but the common flaw in thinking is that the most specific way of training is doing work at race pace or faster than race pace. In theory, it sounds natural to train at a speed that you need to perform at when racing.

The speed you are running will be majorly limited by the aerobic abilities, where the ability to run at a maximum speed at the anaerobic threshold is the main one.

Thus from a physiological standpoint, *actual* specific training is training where you optimize the internal cell processes. The most effective way is not at race speed but is a combination of larger amounts of lactate threshold work with a fair level of total running.

The mechanical “benefit” you get from running faster on sessions, both doing runs a little faster than the threshold plus doing too much training at race pace for longer periods of the year cannot be compared to the increase in performance you’ll get from optimizing the anaerobic threshold.

Lactate  - A brief overview

I recommend taking a look at Questions about Lactate Testing as it helps to explain some of the science and physiology behind lactate. I summarize some of it below.

The body produces energy via two main systems during training/competition: the aerobic and anaerobic systems. Lactate is the output of the anaerobic system; after that it performs its most important function. It is the main fuel for the aerobic system during competition and much of training. Lactate is NOT a waste product. It is a fuel source for the heart, brain and skeletal muscles during strenuous efforts.

Measuring lactate is a way of assessing how strong each energy system is, or essentially how well-conditioned the athlete is at a specific point in time. The more fit you are, the higher level of exercise intensity you will be able to sustain for a given level of lactate production.

Lactate Threshold

        There is a lot of confusion around lactate threshold training. The term and terms associated with it have come to mean lots of things because it has been co-opted by popular running media.

        For this discussion it will be useful to refer to figure 1, as it illustrates the key concepts of lactate training.

        From the graph I’ll also derive some definitions:

  • LT1 is the same as AEROBIC Threshold.
  • LT1 intensity is the intensity you can hold for 3 to 4 hours
  • For moderately trained athletes this is around marathon race pace
  • LT2 refers to ANAEROBIC Threshold.
  • LT2 intensity is the intensity you can hold for 45 - 60 minutes
  • This is an elite runner’s half-marathon pace and the moderately trained runner’s 10km to 10 mile race pace.

Training with Lactate Threshold

As outlined by Marius Bakken and others you can go out and buy a lactate threshold meter to measure lactate during workouts. This is rather expensive, so I prefer to settle for the close heuristics that I outlined above. LT1 is around 3-4 hour intensity and LT2 is around 45 - 60 minutes intensity.

The second tenant in LT training, as is common in most modern training methodologies, is polarization. Here the main idea is that training should be done at the extremes- easy and hard intensities. Moderate intensity is generally excluded in the polarized model. Referring to Figure 1, easy intensity is equal to or below LT1 and hard intensity is at the border of LT2.

For both LT1 and LT2, the way to improve these thresholds is by training at or just below the threshold level. Perhaps most importantly, training at above the threshold does no more to increase it then training at just below it(or right at it). Thus, in most cases, it is detrimental to train above the threshold level that you want to improve. This is because increased intensity increases needed recovery time and injury risk. A corollary of this is that for the shorter threshold work, it’s better to stay very close or at threshold- and maybe even every so slightly above it towards the end. For longer intervals, the goal is to float just below the threshold level for the entire duration of the interval.

Under the lactate threshold training paradigm there are 4 main training intensities. From easiest to hardest they are: Recovery/Easy, LT1, LT2, and above LT2: X-element.

Recovery/Easy

        This is quite self explanatory and is to facilitate recovery between workouts. A good check to see if you are running easy enough is if you can breathe through your nose comfortably.

LT1

The useful heuristic here is a pace that can be sustained for 3-4 hours. For most people, marathon pace[a] is likely a good estimate of this. This is the pace that increases aerobic capacity.

LT2

The vast majority of “hard workouts” are done at LT2. Typically LT2 workouts range from 30 to 60 minutes at most. I’d like to emphasize that there is very little difficulty to any given LT2 workout. This is the pace that increases anaerobic capacity.

Professionals typically perform 4 or more of these sessions per week– in the form of 2 double sessions.

There are generally 3 main types of LT2 workouts: shorter and longer intervals as well as continuous runs. Shorter ones range from 45 seconds to 3 minutes while the longer ones can be up to 20 minutes. Continuous LT2 workouts are usually 25 - 45 minutes in duration.

X-Element

This is different stimuli and usually a higher intensity than LT2.

Implementation:

Threshold paradigm is usually implemented in a weekly manner similar to this:

  • 2 x double threshold session @ LT2
  • Each double day will typically have a short interval LT2 session and a longer or continuous LT2 session.
  • 1 x hill work (X-element)
  • 2 x strength session
  • 1-2 x LT1 session (usually the Long Run is included here)
  • Recovery/Easy runs

The goal here being to maximize time spent at/around LT2 as it is the primary driver of fitness improvement. Less experienced runners will likely only do 1 double threshold(LT2) session and another single threshold(LT2) session as opposed to 2 doubles.

Looking at this framework it can seem as though there is a lot of hardwork and intensity crammed into a single week. However that is why it is so critical to ensure that you do not train at levels higher than would otherwise be expected. When you do an LT2 session, you must ensure that you don’t go over the LT2 threshold. I cannot emphasize this point enough: Any given LT2 session does not feel that difficult. There are very few, truly difficult workouts in the lactate threshold paradigm. Furthermore, recovery days MUST be treated as recovery. This can easily mean no running if the body isn’t up to it.

Supplements to Running

Strides

Strides are a classic way to gain and maintain basic speed and help with turnover. What strides *actually* are vary depending on who you ask, but here are the basic types:

  • Short, explosive sprints, lasting from 10-15 seconds
  • Done uphill or on flat terrain
  • Progressive 100-200 meter sprints(start slower and get faster)
  • Race-pace strides
  • Usually 75-100 meters done at 3k to 5k

Rest can be a walk/jog back to the start. The goal of strides is to elicit muscle fiber recruitment and improve form. You should never strain during strides. They should be done with good form, strong movements and should be comfortable. Strides are typically done 2-3 times per week.

Drills

The goal of drills is to relax, gain neuromuscular coordination and improve running economy. For what it’s worth, short, fast hills usually accomplish the goals of drills more efficiently.

Drills can be a good warm up for hard workouts. Focus on snappy, explosive movements. A good way to do this is to start alternating the drill every other step at walking speed, then slowly increase the speed and start doing it every step at a faster and faster rate.

A quick drill routine:

  1. A skips
  2. High knees
  3. Butt kicks
  4. Carioca 

Strength Training

Strength training is a topic so broad and deep it deserves its own special document. As a whole, strength training has many purposes– rehab, “prehab”, improving running economy, and increasing power-to-weight ratio– to name a few. Depending on your needs and goals, a strength program will be more or less focused on these components. My own view on this is that strength training should be viewed as supplementation and prevention. When compared to the benefits of anaerobic threshold training, strength training will not make you substantially faster. That being said, the runner that is consistent and is rarely injured will almost always be faster than the constantly injured runner. This is where strength training really shines– it will keep you consistent and healthy so that you are able to continue to perform quality workouts. Non-insignificant gains can also be made to running economy through strength training, further improving performance. Typically, runners will have 1-3 strength sessions a week, with the sweet spot being 2 sessions on days with hard workouts.

        In general, I advise a two pronged approach to strength training; Body-weight exercise, and plyometrics. For those who are truly dedicated, maximal weight-lifting is an incredible tool to prevent injury, improve running economy and build strength. If possible it’s a very nice to have, but certainly not a necessity.

Weight Lifting

Disclaimer: Be very careful when lifting heavy weights

Somewhere along the line, it became cool for distance runners to do low-weight, high rep weight lifts as a means to enhance performance. Please don’t do this. It’s at best an inefficient way to train and at worst a waste of time and energy. If you’re going to weight lift, the research is pretty explicit: High weight, low rep, full recovery is the way to go.

For distance runners, I like to keep things very simple. For the upper body a dumbbell bench press or barbell bench press is sufficient. Find a weight you can do 3 sets of 10 reps. If that’s just the bar, then do 3 x 10 x 45lbs and that is upper body weight lifting for the day.

The lower body and posterior chain are a bit more important for runners. Again, keeping things simple, a back squat or hex bar deadlift are good choices. For both I recommend starting with the bar/hex bar and progressively adding weight until all you can manage is about 3 reps at that weight. Then you can build from there. Eventually you might get to a progression of 10 reps-6 reps-6 reps-3 reps. Each time you increase the weight such that you can only accomplish the max number of reps given during the set. Once in a while It’s also good to find an absolute maximum that you can lift. If you plan on lifting two days a week, a good idea is to squat one day and hex-bar deadlift the other. Hex bars deadlifts are pretty simple to figure out– just pick up the weight. Squats can be tricker and more dangerous. Runners also tend to have much less mobility than other people so don’t go super hard on yourself if you can’t make depth. In fact, quarter and half squats are fine and accomplish many of the same benefits.

Sleep

The single most powerful performance enhancing drug you can take is a good night’s sleep. It is the single best and most effective recovery tool you have– by a long shot. If you can lock down a solid sleep schedule you will have a substantial advantage over others.

This is one that I find is often hard to get right– not because it’s a hard thing to understand, but because the implementation is difficult if you have a life. Most people can’t swallow the pill that they need to be in bed by 9:00pm so they can comfortably get a solid 9+ hours of sleep. Frankly this one is so important I think it’s what keeps a lot of athletes from ever reaching their true potential.

Guidelines

  • Keep a consistent sleep schedule – even on weekends(ESPECIALLY on weekends).
  • Even +/- 30 minutes can cause disruptions in sleep schedule.
  • Maximize light exposure in the morning
  • try for sunlight if possible
  • Minimize light exposure when going to bed
  • Particularly blue light exposure
  • Phones are literally the bain of your existence
  • Try to optimize around REM cycles
  • Occur approximately every 90 minutes
  • That’s why 9 hours is a good sleep target, it coincides with the end of a REM cycle

Goal Setting and Planning

I recommend goal setting be done on a few different levels. First, once a year you should sit down and set the goals you have for that year. Then break the year down into seasons, which usually is on a quarterly basis. Then set the goals for that season. Finally, the most fundamental level is weekly organization.

As a general rule of thumb, goals should be actionable and measurable(as much as possible). Saying that you want to run more is not a goal. Saying that you want to increase your weekly mileage from 40 to 50 is a goal.  Note that there is an action that is taken, increasing mileage, and a way to measure that action– you can keep track of how many miles you run. There are some goals that are harder to quantify, so this is not a hard rule, but in general it is a good thing to strive for.

For a template resource to get started consider the training outline provided in the “training outline” section of appendix. I recommend filling this out once per season as a way to focus on, and remind yourself of, goals.

Yearly Goals/Planning

Yearly goals set the framework for how you will approach your entire year. You should return back to them often– it can be a good reminder of why you are doing what you are doing.

In a yearly goal/planning meeting consider the following information:

  • What are my PR’s at each distance from the previous year(s)?
  • How much do I want to improve each one? (and is that reasonable?)
  • What was last year's total mileage?
  • How much do I want to increase it by?
  • What are other places I need to improve on as a whole?
  • How can I improve in these areas?

Weekly Planning

In many ways, weekly structure is the fundamental building block of a running plan. Generally this provides the template that is repeated over and over again during a season. Typically each day is divided into 2 segments- morning and afternoon.

Unlike yearly and seasonal planning, weekly planning is about fitting training into the rest of your life(although, yearly/seasonal does a little of this by setting race dates etc). Assuming you aren’t a professional runner, you likely have other obligations that running simply can’t take priority over. For most people these things work or school.

Weekly planning has two parts: (1) figuring out what training has to be done and the general time it will be done(morning or afternoon), (2) Fitting training into the rest of your life. An example of part 1 is found in table 5. Figure 2 illustrates part 2. A good rule to practice is planning out your week the night before it begins. If you operate on a Monday - Sunday schedule, then Sunday night is a great time to plan out the week.

Training Logs

Keeping detailed logs is very important. They allow you to look back on training. From that you can answer a lot of questions:

  • When I did this workout, I was in this kind of shape
  • How long does it take to recovery from sickness
  • What were some causes of this injury?
  • How will I avoid them in the future?
  • How closely am I keeping to the training plan?
  • Am I overtraining?
  • Am I being honest about my training and how much work I’m doing?

Training logs are also a great way to look back and affirm that you’re on the right track. They can be a big confidence booster after a bad workout/race or in the days leading up to a race. Something that most people forget is that a training log is not just distance, time and pace. It can contain a wide variety of subjective and objective measures.

Final Thoughts

On LT2

I cannot emphasize this enough: LT2 pace is probably slower than you think. Any given LT2 workout is surprisingly easy– do not increase the pace of these workouts because they feel easy. If you do this, then in a month or so you will fall apart. The philosophy of lactate training is basically: Accumulate a lot of volume at a moderately-high effort while ensuring lots of recovery in between.

Training with the lactate paradigm requires immense discipline. Usually not to go slow, but to keep yourself from going too fast on any given day.

On Training in General

The commitment to training super hard is a big one. It requires long hours. Often requires social isolation and potentially ostracization. It can be INCREDIBLY monotonous. Go do 15 x 400 @ LT2 with 45 seconds rest between reps. You might be shocked how long it takes to get 15. Immense sacrifice is a prerequisite for this kind of thing. You have to sleep a lot, usually at the expense of social endeavors. You have to eat incredibly well, which can often mean extensive meal planning. Somedays you just won’t want to, but you have to anyway. Finally, you have to hold yourself accountable to all of this, and more.

Make sure to take a step back and look at the whole picture. Running is a largely aerobic activity. The biggest factor to improving running is running a lot. But also remember that missing a day or two isn’t going to make a big difference. Try to consistently get in as many miles as possible and not worry so much about the short term.
